## CONTENT

<p><strong>Animalism</strong> is one of rock and roll’s great transition albums. Released in November 1966, it was officially the fifth American studio offering by The Animals, yet it represented something far more complicated than a simple addition to their discography. It was the sound of a legendary band shattering under the pressures of fame and relentless touring, only to be immediately, wildly reassembled by its visionary frontman, Eric Burdon.</p>

<p>The original Wikipedia summary, describing <em>Animalism</em> merely as the "fifth American album," fails to capture the inherent drama embedded in its grooves. This record is not just music; it is a historical artifact documenting the exact moment the gritty, R&B-focused British Invasion group died, giving way to the psychedelic, experimental, and ultimately short-lived iteration known as Eric Burdon & The Animals.</p>

<p>For fans and historians trying to chart the band’s course, <em>Animalism</em> is the vital bridge between the foundational blues of tracks like "House of the Rising Sun" and the full-blown lysergic explorations of 1967’s <em>Winds of Change</em>. It captures the chaos, the revolving door of personnel, and the increasingly ambitious musical tastes of Eric Burdon as he dragged the group kicking and screaming into the Age of Aquarius.</p>

<h2>The Great American Discography Discrepancy</h2>

<p>To understand <em>Animalism</em>, one must first grasp the frustrating complexity of The Animals' 1960s release strategy. Unlike their clean-cut British counterparts, The Animals’ UK and US discographies were dramatically different, driven by contractual obligations and the desire of American label MGM Records to rapidly capitalize on the group’s immense popularity.</p>

<p>The album <em>Animalism</em> was, in the truest sense, an American creation. It was **not** released in the UK, where the band was signed to Decca and Pye. Instead, it was assembled by MGM primarily from material recorded throughout 1966 that had not yet appeared on a US LP.</p>

<p><strong>What <em>Animalism</em> actually is:</strong></p>
<ul>
    <li>A collection of late-period singles.</li>
    <li>Tracks recorded during the final sessions featuring original members.</li>
    <li>Newer material showcasing the emerging post-R&B sound.</li>
    <li>Crucially, the album was stitched together just as the original lineup fractured.</li>
</ul>

<p>This method of compilation explains why the sound of the record is so diverse—it features the final contributions of original drummer John Steel alongside tracks with replacement Barry Jenkins, and production spanning multiple sessions under the supervision of acclaimed American producer Tom Wilson.</p>

<h2>The End of an Era: Personnel Shifts of 1966</h2>

<p>The year 1966 marked the total dissolution of The Animals as the world knew them. Following the departure of keyboardist Alan Price in 1965, the band’s internal cohesion rapidly deteriorated. By the time <em>Animalism</em> was being compiled, guitarist Hilton Valentine, bassist Chas Chandler, and drummer John Steel—the instrumental core of the classic lineup—were all either gone or on their way out.</p>

<p>Eric Burdon, however, was resolute in continuing, essentially hijacking the band’s name for his own artistic evolution. The album thus serves as a sonic farewell to the original iteration and a grand introduction to the new direction. The influence of new players is palpable, especially the introduction of <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barry_Jenkins_(drummer)">Barry Jenkins</a> on drums, whose style was more fluid and less traditionally R&B than Steel’s.</p>

<p>It is in this environment of musical upheaval that the album finds its gritty charm. The instrumentation fluctuates, sometimes leaning on session players, sometimes featuring the new, official lineup (Burdon, Jenkins, bassist Chris Curtis, and guitarist Andy Summers—yes, the future guitarist of The Police, though his influence is subtle on this specific release, which relies heavily on older sessions).</p>

<h2>Sound and Style: Embracing the Psychedelic Blues Shift</h2>

<p>If The Animals’ first albums were defined by raw, aggressive electric blues and R&B covers, <em>Animalism</em> heralded a move toward a more sophisticated, though still fundamentally blues-based, aesthetic. This was the moment Burdon realized the confines of traditional R&B could no longer hold his creativity. He started listening deeply to Bob Dylan, the burgeoning psychedelic movement in California, and, most importantly, moving toward original songwriting.</p>

<h3>The Emergence of Original Material</h3>

<p>While their early success was built on brilliant re-interpretations (like Nina Simone’s "Don't Let Me Be Misunderstood"), <em>Animalism</em> sees The Animals flexing their muscles with original compositions, even if the majority of tracks are still covers. This new focus is most evident in the album’s standout tracks, which often carry a darker, more philosophical lyrical edge characteristic of the mid-60s counterculture.</p>

<p>The album effectively blends three distinct sounds:</p>
<ol>
    <li><strong>The Gritty R&B Legacy:</strong> Tracks like the Chuck Berry classic “Too Much Monkey Business” serve as a strong tether to their roots, reminding listeners of their powerful stage presence.</li>
    <li><strong>Folk-Rock Experimentation:</strong> The influence of Dylan and American folk structures begins to creep into the arrangements, offering moments of introspection amidst the usual high-energy numbers.</li>
    <li><strong>Early Psychedelia:</strong> The sound starts to become warped, utilizing studio effects, extended jams, and a fuzzier, more distorted guitar tone—a direct preparation for the acid rock they would soon pursue.</li>
</ol>

<h2>Track-by-Track Deep Dive: The Essential Cuts</h2>

<p>The sequence of <em>Animalism</em>, though assembled by a record label, accidentally tells the story of the band’s breakneck evolution. Several tracks stand out not just as great songs, but as crucial historical footnotes.</p>

<h3>1. “Don’t Bring Me Down”</h3>

<p>This opening track, a major UK and US hit single, is the undisputed centerpiece of <em>Animalism</em> and an essential piece of 1966 pop history. Written by Carole King and Gerry Goffin, it perfectly encapsulated the new direction. It married a driving, almost proto-garage rock beat with a distinctly atmospheric, echoing production that hinted at the psychedelic future. It sounds polished but retains the essential roughness of Burdon’s vocal delivery. The sound is thick and dynamic, showing the band’s increasing capability in the studio.</p>

<h3>2. “See See Rider”</h3>

<p>This traditional folk/blues standard was recorded during the final days of the original lineup, featuring Hilton Valentine’s signature guitar work. It was a successful single for the group, showing that even while experimenting, they could still deliver traditional blues with the punch and authenticity that had defined their initial success. However, its inclusion here contrasts sharply with the newer, stranger tracks.</p>

<h3>3. “Maudie” (A Burdon Masterpiece)</h3>

<p>Originally recorded by John Lee Hooker, The Animals’ version of “Maudie” exemplifies the transitional nature of the album. It is a slow, simmering track dominated by Burdon’s anguished vocal performance. It moves beyond a simple cover, establishing a mood of intense, almost debilitating blues misery. This level of emotional depth and willingness to slow the tempo previewed the dramatic style Burdon would adopt in the years following.</p>

<h3>4. “A Love Like Ours”</h3>

<p>An often-overlooked track on the album, “A Love Like Ours” is a stark reminder of the sophisticated songwriting available to the group at the time. It showcases a growing maturity in their vocal arrangements and moves closer to soul territory than standard blues. Its inclusion solidifies <em>Animalism</em> as a highly diverse listen, far from a cohesive concept album, but compelling due to its variety.</p>

<h2>The Collaborative Ghost: Producers and Uncredited Influences</h2>

<p>The success of <em>Animalism</em>, in terms of sonic quality and compilation coherence, is largely owed to producer Tom Wilson. Wilson, famous for his work with Bob Dylan and Frank Zappa, had a knack for navigating volatile artistic situations and transforming disparate session tapes into marketable albums.</p>

<p>Wilson’s ability to pull tracks from different sessions, featuring different lineups, and still craft an LP that flowed logically is a testament to his expertise. The result is an album that feels less like a traditional studio effort and more like a carefully curated retrospective of a band undergoing a high-speed identity crisis.</p>

<p>Furthermore, rumors and speculation persist regarding the involvement of a young Frank Zappa in the *Animalism* sessions. While Zappa’s direct, credited contribution is generally linked to Burdon’s later *Winds of Change* album, the experimental and sometimes jarring production choices—especially on tracks like "The Other Side of This Life"—carry a definite Mothers of Invention sensibility, suggesting that Burdon was actively absorbing the avant-garde Californian sounds that Zappa championed.</p>

<h2>Legacy and Critical Reassessment</h2>

<p>Upon its release, *Animalism* performed respectably, peaking within the Top 40 of the Billboard 200. Critics at the time noted the inconsistency but praised the high quality of the key singles. However, the album has often been overshadowed by the blockbuster hits of the original lineup and the full-blown psychedelic spectacle of the "New Animals" that followed.</p>

<p>Today, <em>Animalism</em> deserves critical reassessment as more than just a transitional album; it is the definitive document of The Animals’ most chaotic period. It captures the exact moment the traditional British Invasion sound gave way to the experimental demands of the counterculture. For collectors, it represents the final appearance of the original group’s essence, filtered through the emerging LSD-laced musical landscape.</p>

<p>The album showcases the raw nerve of Eric Burdon, a singer determined not to be pigeonholed. By blending classic blues with the nascent sounds of psychedelia, he set the stage for the rest of his career. <em>Animalism</em> is noisy, complex, and sometimes frustratingly disjointed, but it is never dull. It is the sound of a band burning down their past to build a strange, new future—a quintessential product of the revolutionary year 1966.</p>

<h3>The Enduring Impact</h3>

<p>While many of the tracks on this album would be recycled or repurposed in later compilations, their initial presentation on <em>Animalism</em> captures a snapshot of vulnerability and aggressive reinvention. It established Eric Burdon as the sole captain of the ship, capable of piloting The Animals through massive internal storms toward the expansive horizons of global psychedelic rock.</p>
