<h3>The Shingle Style: An American Architectural Revolution</h3>

<p>The Shingle Style flourished between roughly 1880 and 1900, originating primarily in coastal New England communities like the Hamptons and Newport. It was a stylistic rebellion against the highly decorative fussiness of the Queen Anne style and the rigid moralizing of Gothic Revival architecture. The Shingle Style drew inspiration from the simpler colonial homes of early America while incorporating the expansive, integrated planning championed by architects like H. H. Richardson. It was, first and foremost, an architecture of materials and mass.</p>

<p>What defines the Shingle Style?</p>
<ul>
    <li><strong>Surface Uniformity:</strong> The critical feature is the cladding of the entire exterior, often including the roof and dormers, in continuous wood shingles. This treatment creates a unified, almost skin-like surface that flows over the building’s mass.</li>
    <li><strong>Horizontal Emphasis:</strong> Unlike the verticality of Gothic structures, Shingle Style buildings often emphasize horizontal sweep, grounding the structure firmly to the earth.</li>
    <li><strong>Sweeping Forms:</strong> The style favors irregular massing, expansive rooflines, and continuous forms, often blurring the distinction between walls and roofs.</li>
    <li><strong>Lack of High Ornamentation:</strong> Decoration is minimal. The visual appeal comes from the texture of the shingles themselves—their color, cut, and shadows—rather than applied carvings or moldings.</li>
</ul>
