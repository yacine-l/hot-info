<h2>The Formative Years: From Riga to the Lure of the East</h2>

<p>Born in 1899 in Riga (then part of the Russian Empire), Eugenie Peterson was raised in a family of privilege that was quickly dismantled by the Russian Revolution. <p>India in the 1930s was a land of profound contrasts. While Westernized elites embraced modernity, the spiritual landscape remained deeply traditional and largely inaccessible to foreigners, particularly women. Devi found herself immersed in a culture that fascinated her, yet she struggled to find an authentic teacher who could guide her toward the advanced spiritual techniques she sought.</p>

<p>Devi spent years navigating India's diverse spiritual schools. She studied Theosophy, connected with philosophers like Jiddu Krishnamurti, and traveled extensively. <p>It was in Mysore that she heard about Tirumalai Krishnamacharya. Crucially, his students were almost exclusively young boys or men from the Brahmin caste, often focused on improving health for competitive wrestling or military training. <p>Devi's initial attempts to join his small group were repeatedly rebuffed. Krishnamacharya’s answer was always the same: “I don’t teach women.”</p>

This adaptation was the philosophical blueprint that Devi would later use to launch yoga globally.</p>

<p>When Devi and her husband prepared to leave India in 1947, Krishnamacharya gave her his blessing and a crucial mandate: she was authorized to teach his style of yoga. This certification, given to a Western woman, was an unparalleled historical moment. It cemented her place as the essential link between the deep traditions of Indian yoga and the nascent interest in the West.</p>

<p>Indra Devi recognized that a physical studio could only reach so many people. To truly launch a global movement, she needed to make yoga portable and scalable.</p>

<p>Devi was also a pioneer in utilizing media. <p>In the 1960s, Devi expanded her mission, moving away from the spotlight of Hollywood to focus on deeper philosophical work.
