## CONTENT

<p>The history of the 17th century is often dominated by continental power struggles—the ambitions of Louis XIV, the turmoil of the English Civil Wars, and the shifting dynamics of the Holy Roman Empire. Yet, a crucial, defining conflict unfolded far from the battlefields of Europe: a bitter, five-year maritime campaign between the nascent naval superpower of England and the formidable corsairs of North Africa. This was the **Anglo-Algerian War** of 1677 to 1682, a conflict frequently marginalized in textbooks, but one that fundamentally shaped the future of global commerce and tested the resolve of Charles II’s revitalized Royal Navy.</p>

<p>Far from a simple punitive raid, this struggle was a total war declared by the Regency of Algiers against English shipping, driven by economic necessity, sovereign pride, and the escalating desire of London to assert total dominance over the Mediterranean’s critical trade routes. It was a clash between two fundamentally different approaches to maritime power: state-backed naval protection versus state-sanctioned privateering.</p>

<h2>The Mediterranean Chessboard: Setting the Stage for Conflict</h2>

<p>To understand why the <strong>Anglo-Algerian War</strong> erupted, we must first appreciate the volatile geopolitical landscape of the mid-17th century Mediterranean. The sea was not merely a trade thoroughfare; it was a battleground where sovereignty was negotiated in cannon fire.</p>

<h3>The Rise of the Barbary Regencies</h3>

<p>The Barbary States—principally Tripoli, Tunis, and Algiers—were nominally autonomous provinces under the sprawling Ottoman Empire. However, by the 1670s, the Regency of Algiers operated with near-complete independence, governed by a powerful council known as the Diwan and its military chief, the Dey.</p>

<p>Algiers’ economy was inextricably linked to privateering, or ‘corsair activity.’ These well-organized fleets, often referred to pejoratively as "pirates," were in reality naval forces commissioned by the state to capture enemy vessels and enslave their crews for ransom or labor. This system provided immense wealth to the city and served as a powerful defensive deterrent.</p>

<h3>England's Maritime Imperative</h3>

<p>Following the Restoration of the Monarchy in 1660, King Charles II focused heavily on expanding England’s global reach. The success of the East India Company, the burgeoning trade with the Levant (modern Middle East), and the need for secure passage through the Straits of Gibraltar made the safety of the Mediterranean a non-negotiable priority.</p>

<p>England had long attempted to manage the Barbary threat through diplomacy and treaties, often paying substantial ransoms and "tributes" to secure temporary peace. However, these agreements were unstable, frequently collapsing when the powerful corsair captains (the *raïs*) deemed a new English ship a tempting target, thus undermining the authority of the Dey.</p>

<h2>The Seeds of War: From Skirmish to Declaration (1672–1677)</h2>

<p>The five-year gap preceding the official declaration of hostilities was characterized by increasing tension, naval provocation, and aggressive retaliation—the classic anatomy of escalation.</p>

<h3>The 1672 Turning Point: The Battle of Bougie</h3>

<p>The defining precursor to the full-scale war occurred in 1672. The Royal Navy, under the command of Sir Edward Spragge (or sometimes, Sir Thomas Allin, depending on the specific engagement), began to adopt an aggressive posture against the Algerian fleet. Recognizing that defense was insufficient, the English sought to strike at the heart of the corsair infrastructure.</p>

<p>The English squadron successfully tracked a major group of Algerian vessels seeking refuge near Bougie (modern Bejaia). In a decisive action, the Royal Navy delivered a significant blow, defeating and often destroying several key Barbary corsairs.</p>

<h3>The Algerian Response: Captures and Retaliation (1674–1676)</h3>

<p>The Diwan could not allow the defeat near Bougie to stand. Over the next four years, the official policy of Algiers shifted from restrained piracy to systemic retaliation against English interests. The summary documents this crucial period of escalation:</p>
<ul>
    <li><strong>Increased Captures:</strong> Between 1674 and 1676, Algerian corsairs systematically targeted and captured English merchant ships.</li>
    <li><strong>Seizure of Sailors:</strong> Hundreds of English sailors were taken prisoner. These individuals became vital bargaining chips, destined for enslavement until hefty ransoms could be paid by the Crown or charitable organizations back home.</li>
    <li><strong>Diplomatic Breakdown:</strong> Despite English protests and renewed diplomatic missions to secure the release of captives, the Diwan refused to negotiate meaningfully unless the English ceased all aggressive naval patrols.</li>

<p>The seizure of ships and men represented a catastrophic loss for English merchants and applied extreme domestic pressure on Charles II. By early 1677, the situation was untenable. Finally, in a clear statement of defiance and frustration with failed diplomacy, Algiers officially declared war on England.</p>

<h2>A Naval War of Attrition (1677–1682)</h2>

<p>The ensuing five years were marked by a fierce, costly war of attrition, primarily fought at sea. The nature of the <strong>Anglo-Algerian War</strong> dictated that large set-piece battles were rare; instead, the conflict centered on blockades, convoy protection, and daring raids against the North African coast.</p>

<h3>The Royal Navy Strategy: Blockade and Force Projection</h3>

<p>England’s primary strategic goals were simple: cripple the Algerian economy by preventing corsairs from sailing and secure the safe passage of merchant fleets. This required the permanent deployment of substantial Royal Navy squadrons to patrol the Straits of Gibraltar and maintain continuous blockades outside the port of Algiers.</p>

<p>Admiral Arthur Herbert (later the Earl of Torrington) was a prominent figure, overseeing the challenging mission of keeping the port sealed. Blockading Algiers was arduous work. The harbor was heavily fortified, and the constant need for resupply meant the English squadrons were often forced to break off, allowing the swift Algerian galleys to slip through and seize new prizes in the Atlantic.</p>

<h3>The Algerian Strategy: Hit-and-Run Warfare</h3>

<p>The Algerians, under various Deys throughout the period, excelled at asymmetrical warfare. Their galleys and smaller frigates were designed for speed, allowing them to evade the heavier, slower English ships-of-the-line. They exploited the vastness of the sea, striking at vulnerable, isolated merchant convoys and quickly retreating to friendly ports or shallow waters where the larger English vessels could not follow.</p>

<p>The war severely impacted global insurance markets. English ships sailing to the Levant or the Americas had to pay astronomical rates for protection, forcing the government to prioritize naval resources solely toward convoy duty, often at the expense of other European commitments.</p>

<h3>Key Engagements and Bombardments</h3>

<p>While the war lacked a single decisive Trafalgar-style engagement, the English did attempt to use the threat of force directly against the city itself. Naval bombardments of Algiers were carried out, aiming to destroy the moored corsair fleets and pressure the Diwan into suing for peace. These attacks, while spectacular, rarely achieved lasting strategic impact, as the city’s defensive batteries were robust and the harbor offered natural protection.</p>

<p>The constant pressure, however, began to take its toll. The sustained Royal Navy presence meant fewer prizes were being brought back to Algiers, leading to internal dissent and putting pressure on the ruling military class.</p>

<h2>Resolution and The Cost of Peace (1682)</h2>

<p>By 1681, both sides recognized the diminishing returns of continued conflict. England had tied up significant naval resources and suffered heavy losses in merchant tonnage and manpower. Algiers, facing economic collapse due to the effective blockades and suffering from internal political instability related to the lack of successful corsair activity, was ready to talk.</p>

<h3>The Treaty of 1682</h3>

<p>In 1682, the **Anglo-Algerian War** officially concluded with the signing of a new peace treaty. The terms reflected the reality that England had established a clear, though costly, naval superiority:</p>
<ul>
    <li><strong>Prisoner Exchange:</strong> A comprehensive agreement was reached for the release and ransom of English captives, though the exact number released remains debated.</li>
    <li><strong>Indemnities:</strong> Algiers agreed to pay reparations for past seizures, though execution of these payments was often slow or incomplete.</li>
    <li><strong>Recognition of Sovereignty:</strong> Crucially, the treaty stipulated that English ships bearing the King’s pass (a recognized standard document) were immune from capture.</li>
    <li><strong>Consular Presence:</strong> England secured the right to maintain a permanent Consul in Algiers, offering a vital link for communication, trade, and defense of English interests.</li>
</ul>

<h3>The Deeper Significance of the Conflict</h3>

<p>The <strong>Anglo-Algerian War</strong> of 1677–1682 was not the final word in Anglo-Barbary relations; conflicts would flare up again in the 1680s and beyond. However, the war marked a fundamental shift in strategy. England had demonstrated a willingness and capability to engage in sustained overseas warfare to protect commercial interests, setting a powerful precedent for its emerging imperial policy.</p>

<p>For Algiers, the war demonstrated the limitations of their privateering model against a unified, professional, and well-funded state navy. While corsair activity continued, the balance of power had begun its irreversible shift toward the European naval powers. The conflict served as an expensive, five-year lesson that maritime safety was not a matter of treaty but a product of overwhelming force.</p>

<p>The war cemented the idea within the Admiralty that the Royal Navy was not merely a defensive force, but an instrument of global economic policy. Every cannonball fired during the struggle against the Barbary corsairs contributed directly to the construction of a maritime order that would eventually see Britain dominate the global seas for centuries.</p>
